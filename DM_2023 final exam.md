## Q1 A. Discuss difference between Risk and Hazard with relevant examples (5 marks)

| Parameter | Hazard | Risk |
|-----------|--------|------|
| **Definition** | Dangerous condition or event that threatens life, property, environment | Probability of loss or damage when hazard affects vulnerable population |
| **Nature** | Potential threat | Likelihood of consequences |
| **Existence** | Can exist without causing harm | Exists only when hazard meets vulnerable elements |
| **Formula** | – | Risk = (Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability) / Capacity |
| **Example 1** | **Earthquake fault line** in Himalayas (hazard exists even if no one lives there) | **Risk of building collapse** in that area if people live in weak structures |
| **Example 2** | **Floodplain of Ganga river** (hazard of flooding exists naturally) | **Risk to villages** built on floodplain during monsoon |

**Key difference:** Hazard is the *threat*; Risk is the *chance of damage* from that threat.

## Q3 A. Discuss with neat sketch formation of Cyclone and elaborate each component (10 marks)

**Sketch of Cyclone (text description):**

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
                    │         OUTER RAINBANDS          │
                    │    ┌─────────────────────┐       │
                    │    │     EYEWALL         │       │
                    │    │  ┌─────────────┐    │       │
                    │    │  │    EYE      │    │       │
                    │    │  │ (Calm, 20-  │    │       │
                    │    │  │  50 km)     │    │       │
                    │    │  └─────────────┘    │       │
                    │    │ (Strongest winds,   │       │
                    │    │  heaviest rain)     │       │
                    │    └─────────────────────┘       │
                    │   (Spiral bands, decreasing      │
                    │    rain intensity outward)        │
                    └─────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Cyclone Formation process:**

1. Warm ocean water (≥26.5°C) evaporates, rises
2. Rising air creates low pressure area
3. Surrounding air rushes in (Coriolis force causes rotation)
4. Rising air cools, condenses, releases latent heat
5. Heat release intensifies low pressure → self-sustaining cycle
6. Wind speed increases → organized structure develops

**Cyclone Components (in detail):**

| Component | Description | Characteristics |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Eye** | Central calm area | 20-50 km diameter; clear skies; light winds; lowest pressure |
| **Eyewall** | Ring of intense thunderstorms around eye | Strongest winds (120+ km/h); heaviest rainfall; most destructive |
| **Rainbands** | Spiral bands extending outward | Decreasing intensity; heavy rain and moderate winds |
| **Eye wall replacement cycle** | New eyewall forms outside old one | Can intensify or weaken cyclone |

**Formation requirements:**
- Sea surface temperature >26.5°C (up to 60m depth)
- Coriolis force (at least 5° from equator)
- Low vertical wind shear (<10 m/s)
- Pre-existing disturbance (low pressure system)
- High humidity in lower to mid-troposphere

## Q4 B. Explain case study of "Nisarg Cyclone" with causes and stages of disaster management (10 marks)

**Case Study: Cyclone Nisarga (3 June 2020)**

**Causes:**

| Cause | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|

| **Low wind shear** | Favourable atmospheric conditions for intensification |
| **Climate change** | Arabian Sea cyclones increasing in frequency due to warming |
| **Pre-existing low pressure** | Low pressure system in east-central Arabian Sea intensified |

**Cyclone Details:**
- Landfall: Near Alibag (Raigad district, Maharashtra), 100 km south of Mumbai

**Stages of Disaster Management:**

| Stage | Actions |
|-------|---------|

- Displaced: 9,000+ evacuated

| **Response** | **Real-time flood mapping** – satellite imagery (SAR) during/after cloud cover shows flooded areas | Kerala (2018) – SAR images mapped inundation even during monsoon clouds |
| **Response** | **Rescue coordination** – map stranded persons (crowdsourced via social media) and NDRF team locations | Mumbai (2005) – GIS could have coordinated rescue boats |

| **Recovery** | **Reconstruction planning** – relocate vulnerable structures out of floodplains | Use GIS floodplain maps for new building permits |

**Case Study: Chennai Floods (2015)**
- GIS used by Tamil Nadu government to:
  - Map Adyar and Cooum river floodplains
  - Identify encroached areas (post-flood removal)
  - Plan evacuation routes and relief camps
  - Assess damage to infrastructure (roads, power lines)

**Formula used in GIS for flash flood prediction:**
```
Q = C × I × A
Q = Peak runoff (cumecs)
C = Runoff coefficient (depends on land use – urban: 0.7-0.9, rural: 0.3-0.5)
I = Rainfall intensity (mm/hr)
A = Catchment area (km²)
```
GIS integrates land use maps (C values), rainfall radar (I), and DEM-derived catchments (A) to predict runoff hotspots.

## Q7 B. Create a checklist of any 10 Do's to avoid Industrial Chemical Accidents with relevant examples (10 marks)

**Checklist of 10 Do's to avoid Industrial Chemical Accidents:**

| S.No | Do's | Explanation | Example (Bhopal reference) |
|------|------|-------------|----------------------------|
| 1 | **Conduct Hazard and Operability (HAZOP) study** | Identify all possible failure points in process | Bhopal – no proper hazard study before operations |
| 2 | **Install multiple redundant safety systems** | Scrubbers, flare towers, refrigeration, water curtains – all should be functional | Bhopal – scrubber off, flare tower under repair, refrigeration off |
| 3 | **Train all workers on emergency procedures** | Regular mock drills; know how to respond to leaks | Bhopal – inadequately trained personnel |
| 4 | **Conduct regular preventive maintenance** | Weekly/monthly inspection of valves, pipes, storage tanks | Bhopal – poor maintenance, leaking valves |
| 5 | **Maintain safety equipment at all times** | Never turn off or bypass safety systems | Bhopal – safety systems deliberately turned off to save costs |
| 6 | **Install gas detection sensors with alarms** | Early warning before leak becomes dangerous | Bhopal – no functioning gas detection |
| 7 | **Prepare on-site emergency plan** | Written plan for immediate response (evacuation, containment, medical) | Bhopal – no emergency plan existed |
| 8 | **Prepare off-site emergency plan with community** | Coordinate with local administration, hospitals, police; train community on "cover nose, move upwind" | Bhopal – community had no warning or training |
| 9 | **Site hazardous industries away from population** | Buffer zone of 5-10 km; no residential areas near chemical plants | Bhopal – plant in densely populated area (violation of norms) |
| 10 | **Conduct regular safety audits by third parties** | Independent audits to identify gaps; enforce findings | Bhopal – no independent audits; Union Carbide self-regulated |

**Additional Do's (if needed):**
- Use **Material Safety Data Sheets (MSDS)** for all chemicals
- Provide **Personal Protective Equipment (SCBA, gas masks)** to workers
- **Public disclosure** of hazards to nearby communities (Right to Know)
