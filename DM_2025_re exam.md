### Q3 A. Illustrate with neat sketch any 2 types of waves causing earthquake and any 2 types of earthquakes (10 marks)

**Sketch 1: Seismic Waves**

```
Body Waves (travel inside Earth)
├── P-waves (Primary) – compressional, fastest, solids+liquids
└── S-waves (Secondary) – transverse, slower, solids only

Surface Waves (travel on Earth’s surface)
├── Love waves – horizontal shaking, most destructive
└── Rayleigh waves – rolling motion
```

**Two types of waves causing earthquake (in detail):**

| Wave Type | Characteristics | Speed | Medium |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|--------|
| **P-waves** | Compressional (push-pull), first to arrive, least destructive | Fastest (1.5-8 km/s) | Solids + liquids |
| **S-waves** | Transverse (side-to-side), second to arrive, more destructive | Slower | Solids only |

**Sketch 2: Types of Earthquakes**

```
Types of Earthquakes
├── Tectonic (most common)
├── Volcanic
├── Collapse
└── Explosive (man-made)
```

**Two types of earthquakes (in detail):**

| Type | Cause | Characteristics | Example |
|------|-------|-----------------|---------|
| **Tectonic Earthquake** | Movement of tectonic plates along faults | Most common, can be very destructive | 2001 Bhuj (7.7), 2004 Indian Ocean (9.1) |
| **Volcanic Earthquake** | Movement of magma beneath volcano | Smaller magnitude, localized, occurs before/after eruption | Barren Island volcano (Andaman) – minor tremors |

### Q4 A. Analysing "Uttarakhand Forest Fires", elaborate how Warning Systems and Drone Technology can be adopted (10 marks)

**Case Study: Uttarakhand Forest Fires (2016)** – 4,500+ hectares burned, 90% human-caused.

**Warning Systems Adoption:**

| Warning Type | How it helps | Example from Uttarakhand |
|--------------|--------------|--------------------------|
| **Satellite-based fire detection** | FSI uses SNPP-VIIRS/MODIS – detects heat anomalies in real-time | After 2016, FSI now sends SMS alerts to forest officers within 30 minutes |
| **IMD fire weather index** | Forecasts fire danger based on temperature, humidity, wind | Red alert when conditions are extreme (temp >40°C, humidity <20%, wind >20 km/h) |
| **Community-based warning** | Local volunteers inform villagers via WhatsApp, phone trees | Village forest protection committees in Garhwal now trained |
| **Automated weather stations** | Real-time data on temperature, wind speed, soil moisture | Installed in Corbett and Rajaji National Park |

**Drone Technology Adoption:**

| Drone Application | How it helps | Example |
|-------------------|--------------|---------|
| **Early detection** | Drones with thermal cameras fly over forests to spot hotspots before they spread | Could have detected small fires in remote areas of Pauri within minutes |
| **Real-time mapping** | Create live fire perimeter maps to guide ground teams | Firefighters know exactly where to go instead of searching blindly |
| **Aerial suppression** | Drones with water balloons or fire retardant can douse small fires | Useful in inaccessible steep slopes of Kedarnath wildlife sanctuary |
| **Post-fire assessment** | Drone imagery maps burned area, identifies hotspots still burning underground | After 2016, used to estimate damage and plan reforestation |

### Q4 B. Critically evaluate five preparedness measures for mitigating effects of earthquake and droughts (10 marks)

**Earthquake Preparedness Measures:**

| Measure | Evaluation (Effectiveness + Challenges) |
|---------|------------------------------------------|
| 1. Earthquake-resistant building codes | **Effective** – Gujarat rebuilt Bhuj with seismic codes, reduced damage in later tremors. **Challenge** – Enforcement in rural areas poor. |
| 2. Retrofitting old structures | **Effective** – Strengthens existing buildings. **Challenge** – Expensive; many owners cannot afford. |
| 3. Public awareness & drills ("Drop, Cover, Hold") | **Effective** – Reduces injuries. **Challenge** – Low participation in schools, offices. |
| 4. Early warning systems (Japan-style) | **Limited** – Earthquake prediction not possible; warning only seconds before. **Challenge** – Expensive infrastructure. |
| 5. Land-use zoning (avoid fault lines) | **Effective** – Prevents construction on most dangerous zones. **Challenge** – Political pressure to allow development. |

**Drought Preparedness Measures:**

| Measure | Evaluation |
|---------|------------|
| 1. Rainwater harvesting | **Highly effective** – Recharges groundwater. **Challenge** – Requires community participation. |
| 2. Watershed management (check dams, percolation tanks) | **Effective** – Increases water availability. **Challenge** – Long-term maintenance needed. |
| 3. Drought-resistant crops (millets, pulses) | **Effective** – Reduces crop failure. **Challenge** – Farmer preference for high-yield water-intensive crops (sugarcane, paddy). |
| 4. Efficient irrigation (drip, sprinkler) | **Effective** – Saves 50-70% water. **Challenge** – High initial cost, subsidies needed. |
| 5. Water rationing & demand management | **Effective** – During acute shortage. **Challenge** – Social resistance, enforcement difficult. |

**Critical Conclusion:** Both earthquakes and droughts require **structural + non-structural** measures. Earthquakes need **enforcement** of codes; droughts need **behavior change** in water use.

### Q5 B. Explain how global climate change is linked to increasing frequency and intensity of floods and droughts (10 marks)

**Mechanism:** Climate change → higher temperatures → more evaporation → altered precipitation patterns → extreme events.

**For Floods:**

| Link | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Warmer air holds more moisture | 7% more moisture per 1°C increase → heavier rainfall | **2013 Uttarakhand** – 375 mm in 24 hours (cloudburst + GLOF) |
| Increased intensity of cyclones | Warmer oceans fuel stronger cyclones → more storm surge flooding | **2019 Cyclone Fani** – Very severe, caused coastal flooding in Odisha |
| Glacial melt (GLOF) | Himalayan glaciers melting → glacial lakes burst → flash floods | **2021 Chamoli flood** – triggered by rock/ice avalanche from glacier melt |
| Urbanization + extreme rain | Impervious surfaces increase runoff | **2018 Kerala floods** – 300% above normal rainfall linked to climate change |

**For Droughts:**

| Link | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Higher temperatures increase evaporation | Dries out soil, reduces water availability | **2016 Maharashtra drought** – consecutive years of deficit rainfall + high evaporation |
| Erratic monsoon patterns | Delayed onset, early withdrawal, long dry spells | **2015-16 Telangana drought** – monsoon failed multiple years |
| El Niño intensification | Climate change may strengthen ENSO → weaker Indian monsoon | **2002 nationwide drought** – severe El Niño year |
| Reduced snowpack | Less snowmelt water for rivers in summer | Himalayan region – affects Ganga basin water availability |

**Conclusion:** Climate change acts as a **threat multiplier** – it makes wet areas wetter (intense floods) and dry areas drier (severe droughts). India is a "hotspot" for both.

### Q6 B. Enlist and explain any 8 Ethical Principles applied in response phase of Disaster Management (10 marks)

| S.No | Ethical Principle | Explanation in Response Phase |
|------|------------------|-------------------------------|
| 1 | **Humanity** | Treat all disaster victims humanely with respect, compassion. Prioritize life saving over all other concerns. |
| 2 | **Impartiality** | Provide relief based on need alone – no discrimination by religion, caste, gender, political affiliation. |
| 3 | **Neutrality** | Do not take sides in political, religious, or ideological conflicts. Focus only on saving lives. |
| 4 | **Non-discrimination** | Equal treatment regardless of age, disability, ethnicity, socioeconomic status. Special attention to most vulnerable. |
| 5 | **Dignity** | Respect privacy and cultural practices of victims. Avoid humiliating practices (e.g., public distribution without privacy). |
| 6 | **Solidarity** | Nations and communities cooperate to help victims. Share resources, expertise. |
| 7 | **Do no harm** | Avoid actions that could worsen situation. Ensure relief materials are safe, appropriate. |

**Source:** These are derived from International Federation of Red Cross (IFRC) Code of Conduct and UN Guiding Principles.
