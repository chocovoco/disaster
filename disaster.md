# Disaster Management - Complete Unit Wise Notes

## UNIT 1: DEFINITION AND TYPES OF DISASTER

---

### HAZARDS

**Definition:** A hazard is a dangerous condition or event that threatens or has the potential for causing injury to life, damage to property, or harm to the environment.

**Key Points:**
- Hazards are potential threats that may or may not occur
- They become disasters only when they affect vulnerable populations
- Hazards can be natural, man-made, or socio-natural

**Types of Hazards:**

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Natural Hazard | Caused by natural phenomena | Cyclones, tsunamis, earthquakes, volcanic eruptions |
| Man-made Hazard | Due to human negligence | Industrial explosions, toxic waste leakage, dam failure, wars |
| Socio-natural Hazard | Both natural and man-made causes | Landslides, floods, droughts, fires |

**Measurement of Hazards:**
- Frequency - How often it occurs
- Magnitude - Size/strength of the event
- Duration - How long the hazard lasts
- Speed of onset - How quickly it develops
- Areal extent - Geographic area affected

---

### DISASTER

**Definition:** A disaster is a serious disruption in the functioning of a community or society causing widespread material, economic, social, or environmental losses that exceed the affected society's ability to cope using its own resources.

**Key Points:**
- Disaster = Hazard + Vulnerability
- The affected community cannot handle it alone
- Requires external assistance

**Causes of Occurrence:**
1. Natural triggers (earthquakes, heavy rainfall, volcanic eruptions)
2. Human negligence (poor construction, improper safety measures)
3. Technological failures (equipment malfunction, design flaws)
4. Environmental degradation (deforestation, climate change)
5. Lack of preparedness and awareness

**Difference between Hazard and Disaster:**

| Parameter | Hazard | Disaster |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Nature | Threat or potential | Actual event causing loss |
| Population | Can occur in less populated areas | Mainly in overpopulated areas |
| Prevention | Can be prevented | Result of multiple conditions |
| Status | Dormant threat | Active destructive event |
| Impact | Poses threat to life/property | Causes widespread destruction |

---

### RISK

**Definition:** Risk is the probability that a particular threat will exploit a particular vulnerability, or the potential loss of life, injury, or destroyed/damaged assets which could occur to a system, society, or community in a specific period of time.

**Disaster Risk Formula:**

```
Disaster Risk = (Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability) / Capacity (Resilience)
```

Where:
- **Hazard** = Potentially damaging physical event
- **Exposure** = People, infrastructure, or assets present in hazard-prone areas
- **Vulnerability** = How susceptible exposed elements are to damage
- **Capacity** = Ability to anticipate, cope with, resist, and recover

**Risk = Probability × Impact**

**Levels of Risk:**

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Acceptable Risk | Extent to which disaster risk is deemed tolerable based on social, economic, political, cultural conditions |
| Residual Risk | Disaster risk that remains even after effective risk reduction measures are in place |

**Risk Management Components:**
- Risk identification
- Risk assessment
- Risk analysis
- Risk evaluation
- Risk reduction
- Risk transfer (insurance)

---

### VULNERABILITY IN DISASTERS

**Definition:** Vulnerability is the degree to which a system, community, or individual is susceptible to, and unable to cope with, the adverse effects of a hazard.

**Concepts of Vulnerability:**

**Types of Vulnerability:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Physical Vulnerability | Building quality, location, infrastructure condition |
| Social Vulnerability | Age, gender, education, health status |
| Economic Vulnerability | Income level, livelihood security, access to resources |
| Environmental Vulnerability | Ecosystem degradation, land use patterns |
| Institutional Vulnerability | Governance quality, preparedness level, response capacity |

**Progression of Vulnerability:**
- Root causes (limited access to resources, power structures)
- Dynamic pressures (lack of training, rapid urbanization)
- Unsafe conditions (fragile buildings, lack of warning systems)
- Disaster occurrence

### NATURAL AND MAN-MADE DISASTERS

**Natural Disasters:** Disasters caused by natural processes and phenomena of the Earth

**Man-made Disasters:** Disasters resulting from human actions, negligence, or technological failures

**Difference between Natural and Man-made Disasters:**

| Parameter | Natural Disasters | Man-made Disasters |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Cause | Natural phenomena | Human negligence/action |
| Prediction | Sometimes possible | Often preventable |
| Frequency | Regular occurrence | Can be reduced by safety measures |
| Examples | Earthquakes, floods, cyclones | Bhopal gas tragedy, Chernobyl |
| Warning time | Sometimes available | Often sudden |

### NATURAL DISASTERS

#### 1. EARTHQUAKES

**Introduction:** An earthquake is a sudden shaking of the Earth's surface caused by the release of energy from tectonic plate movements. This energy radiates in the form of seismic waves, leading to ground tremors.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Sudden ground shaking
- Rumbling sound
- Visible waves on ground surface
- Cracks in buildings and roads
- Falling objects

**Measurement:**
- **Magnitude:** Measured by Richter Scale or Moment Magnitude Scale (energy released)
- **Intensity:** Measured by Modified Mercalli Scale (damage observed)

**Seismic Zones of India:**

| Zone | Risk Level | Areas |
|------|------------|-------|
| Zone V | Very High Risk | Himalayan region, Northeast states, Rann of Kutch |
| Zone IV | High Risk | Delhi, Jammu & Kashmir, Bihar, parts of Maharashtra |
| Zone III | Moderate Risk | Gujarat, Goa, Kerala, Tamil Nadu |
| Zone II | Low Risk | Remaining parts of India |

**Causes:**
- Movement of tectonic plates
- Volcanic activity
- Collapse of underground caverns
- Artificial explosions
- Movement along fault lines

**Effects/Impacts:**
- Physical: Ground shaking, building collapse, landslides, tsunamis, liquefaction, fires
- Economic: Property loss, infrastructure damage, increased living costs
- Social: Deaths, injuries, displacement, psychological trauma
- Environmental: Soil damage, habitat destruction, water changes

**Basic Terminology:**
- **Focus (Hypocenter):** Point inside Earth where earthquake starts
- **Epicenter:** Point on Earth's surface directly above focus
- **Depth of focus:** How deep the earthquake starts
  - Shallow: <70 km
  - Intermediate: 70-300 km
  - Deep: >300 km
- **Epicentral distance:** Distance from epicenter to observation point

**Faults and Their Significance:**
- **Fault line:** Line where fault appears on Earth's surface
- **Fault plane:** Actual surface on which rocks slip
- **Fault scarp:** Small cliff formed due to movement

**Types of Faults:**
| Fault Type | Movement | Forces |
|------------|----------|--------|
| Normal Fault | Hanging wall moves down | Tension/Extensional |
| Reverse Fault | Hanging wall moves up | Compression |
| Strike-slip Fault | Horizontal movement | Shearing |
| Oblique-slip Fault | Combination of vertical and horizontal | Mixed |

**Seismic Waves:**

| Wave Type | Speed | Medium | Nature |
|-----------|-------|--------|--------|
| P-waves (Primary) | Fastest | Solids + liquids | Compressional/Longitudinal |
| S-waves (Secondary) | Slower | Solids only | Transverse |
| Surface waves (Love & Rayleigh) | Slowest | Earth's surface | Most destructive |

**Prediction Signs:**
- Unusual animal behavior
- Water level changes in wells
- Foreshocks before major earthquake
- Temperature changes
- Ground uplift
- Change in seismic wave velocity

**Earthquake Management in India:**
- **NDMA:** Sets disaster management policies
- **SDMA:** Creates and implements disaster plans at state level
- **National Seismological Network:** Monitors earthquake activities
- **BIS Seismic Zonation Map (2025):** Introduced new Zone VI for entire Himalayan arc

#### 2. FLOODS

**Introduction:** A flood is the overflow of water onto normally dry land, usually caused by excess rainfall, river overflow, dam failure, or storm surges.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Rising water levels in rivers/streams
- Waterlogging in low-lying areas
- Overflow of drainage systems
- Submersion of land

**Causes:**

| Natural Causes | Human Causes |
|----------------|---------------|
| Heavy/continuous rainfall | Deforestation |
| Cloudburst | Urbanization (concrete surfaces) |
| Snowmelt | Poor drainage systems |
| Cyclones/storm surges | Encroachment of floodplains |
| River blockage (landslides/ice jams) | Dam mismanagement |

**Types of Floods:**

| Type | Description | Duration |
|------|-------------|----------|
| River Flood | Overflow of rivers due to heavy rainfall/snowmelt | Days to weeks |
| Flash Flood | Sudden flooding within minutes/hours | Hours |
| Coastal Flood | Caused by cyclones, storm surges, tsunamis | Hours to days |
| Urban Flood | Occurs in cities due to poor drainage | Hours to days |
| Dam Breach Flood | Dam failure releasing water suddenly | Sudden, short duration |

**Preparedness - Do's and Don'ts:**

**Before Flood:**
- Prepare emergency kit with food, water, medicines
- Keep documents in waterproof bags
- Know safe routes to nearest shelter
- Stay updated with news
- Store ready-to-eat food for at least a week

**During Flood:**
- Do not walk or enter flood water
- Stay away from electric poles and wires
- Eat clean food, drink boiled/chlorinated water
- Avoid open drains and sewerage lines
- Move to higher ground

**After Flood:**
- Do not enter damaged areas
- Do not drink tap water until declared safe
- Stay away from electrical equipment if wet
- Be careful of snakes and insects
- Use mosquito nets to prevent malaria

**Response and Recovery:**
- Immediate rescue operations
- Provision of medical aid
- Supply of drinking water and food
- Setting up relief camps
- Infrastructure repair
- Rehabilitation of affected people
- Financial compensation

**Flood Management in India:**

**Structural Measures:**
| Measure | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| Reservoirs/Dams | Store excess water, release controlled flow |
| Embankments/Levees | Physical barrier against floodwater |
| Drainage improvement | Prevent water accumulation |
| Channelization | Increase river carrying capacity |
| Flood diversion | Redirect water to less risky areas |

**Non-Structural Measures:**
- Flood forecasting and early warning
- Floodplain zoning
- Land use planning
- Flood insurance
- Public awareness

**Key Agencies:**
- **CWC (Central Water Commission):** Flood forecasting network
- **NDMA:** Policy and guidelines
- **IMD:** Weather monitoring and rainfall prediction

#### 3. DROUGHTS

**Introduction:** A drought is a prolonged period of abnormally low rainfall leading to water shortage. It is a slow-onset disaster that develops gradually over time.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Decrease in rainfall below normal
- Falling groundwater levels
- Drying of rivers and lakes
- Crop failure
- Water scarcity

**Causes:**

| Natural Causes | Human Causes |
|----------------|---------------|
| Low/irregular rainfall | Deforestation |
| High pressure systems | Overuse of groundwater |
| Climate variability (El Nino) | Poor water management |
| Change in wind patterns | Urbanization |
| High temperature → evaporation | Pollution |

**Types of Drought:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Meteorological Drought | Rainfall significantly below normal for a region |
| Hydrological Drought | Water levels in rivers, lakes, and groundwater decrease |
| Agricultural Drought | Soil moisture insufficient for crops |
| Socio-economic Drought | Water shortage affects people, economy, supply-demand balance |

**Meteorological Drought Classification (IMD):**
- **Slight drought:** 11-25% less than normal rainfall
- **Moderate drought:** 26-50% less than normal
- **Severe drought:** More than 50% less than normal

**Drought Indicators:**
- Rainfall deficiency percentage
- Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI)
- Palmer Drought Severity Index (PDSI)
- Groundwater level decline
- Soil moisture content
- Crop health condition

**Preparedness - Do's and Don'ts:**
- Store water efficiently
- Use water judiciously
- Implement rainwater harvesting
- Grow drought-resistant crops
- Follow government advisories on water usage

**Response and Recovery:**
- Supply drinking water through tankers
- Provide food and relief materials
- Employment programs (MGNREGA)
- Medical support for affected people
- Fodder for livestock
- Financial assistance to farmers
- Crop insurance claims

**Drought Management in India:**

**Mitigation Measures:**
| Category | Measures |
|----------|----------|
| Water Management | Rainwater harvesting, watershed management, efficient irrigation (drip/sprinkler) |
| Agricultural Practices | Drought-resistant crops, mulching, contour cultivation |
| Land Management | Afforestation, soil conservation, crop rotation |
| Policy Measures | Water conservation laws, crop insurance schemes |

**Drought Prone Areas in India:**
- Rajasthan
- Gujarat
- Maharashtra (especially Marathwada)
- Karnataka
- Parts of Andhra Pradesh and Telangana

**Government Initiatives:**
- NDMA drought monitoring systems
- Irrigation schemes
- Crop insurance schemes
- Drought management plans

#### 4. LANDSLIDES

**Introduction:** A landslide is the downward movement of soil, rocks, or debris from a slope due to gravity. It occurs when the stability of a slope is disturbed.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Cracks on ground or walls
- Tilting of trees or poles
- Sudden change in water flow
- Unusual sounds (cracking, rumbling)
- Bulging ground at slope base

**Causes:**

| Natural Causes | Human Causes |
|----------------|---------------|
| Heavy rainfall (most common) | Deforestation |
| Earthquakes | Mining and quarrying |
| Volcanic activity | Road construction |
| Weathering of rocks | Overloading slopes |
| Erosion by rivers/glaciers | Poor drainage |

**Types of Landslides:**

| Type | Description | Movement |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Falls | Rock/soil falling from steep cliff | Free fall |
| Slides | Mass moves along defined surface | Sliding |
| - Rotational Slide | Curved slip surface (spoon-shaped) | Rotational |
| - Translational Slide | Planar/flat surface | Linear |
| Flows | Material flows like liquid | Fluid-like |
| - Mud Flow | Wet material with sand/silt/clay | Flow |
| - Debris Flow | Loose soil, rocks, organic matter + water | Rapid flow |
| Creep | Very slow movement over time | Imperceptible |
| Debris Avalanche | Rapid flow of mixed material | Very fast |
| Block Slide | Large slab moving as cohesive unit | Translational |
| Rock Fall | Rock suddenly falling from steep cliff | Free fall |
| Topple | Forward rotation around a pivot | Rotational |
| Lateral Spread | Sideways movement over liquefied layer | Horizontal |

**Landslide Mechanism:**
- Landslides occur when: **Driving forces (gravity) > Resisting forces (friction + cohesion)**
- Water increases weight and reduces friction → slope failure

**Preparedness - Do's and Don'ts:**
- Avoid construction on steep slopes
- Plant trees on upper slopes
- Do not block natural water flow
- Identify risk zones before building
- Move to safe places if warning signs appear

**Response and Recovery:**
- Immediate evacuation
- Search and rescue operations
- Medical assistance
- Clearing debris from roads
- Restoring transportation
- Financial assistance for affected families
- Rehabilitation of displaced people

**Landslide Management in India:**

**Mitigation Measures:**
| Type | Measures |
|------|----------|
| Structural | Retaining walls, rock bolts, drainage systems, slope grading |
| Non-structural | Afforestation, land use planning, early warning systems |
| Community | Awareness, disaster preparedness, evacuation protocols |

**Government Frameworks:**
- **Disaster Management Act, 2005:** Legal framework
- **NDMA Guidelines (2009):** Steps to reduce landslide risks
- **National Landslide Risk Management Strategy (2019):** Covers hazard mapping, monitoring, early warning

**Landslide Prone Areas in India:**
- Himalayan region (J&K, Himachal, Uttarakhand) - 66.5%
- North-East India (50% of total prone areas)
- Western Ghats (Maharashtra, Kerala, Karnataka, Goa, Tamil Nadu) - 14.7%
- Nilgiri hills
- Araku region in Andhra Pradesh (Eastern Ghats)

**Pre-disaster Studies:**
- Landslide hazard zonation mapping (macro and meso scale)
- Slope stability assessment
- Afforestation of upper slopes
- Preserving existing vegetation
- Avoiding construction in risk zones

**Post-disaster Studies:**
- Developing inventory databases
- Quick response to landslide incidences
- Assessment of potentially dangerous landslides
- Maintaining records for future study

#### 5. LAND SUBSIDENCE

**Introduction:** Land subsidence is the gradual sinking or sudden settling of the Earth's surface due to underground material movement or removal.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Sinking ground surface
- Cracks in buildings and roads
- Formation of depressions
- Tilting of structures

**Causes:**
- Groundwater extraction (excessive pumping)
- Mining activities
- Natural compaction of sediments
- Oil and gas extraction
- Underground construction
- Earthquake shaking

**Vulnerable Areas in India:**
- Parts of Delhi (excessive groundwater extraction)
- Kolkata (natural sediment compaction)
- Parts of Gujarat and Rajasthan

**Preparedness and Mitigation:**
- Regulate groundwater extraction
- Monitor subsidence-prone areas
- Building codes for subsidence zones
- Rainwater harvesting to recharge groundwater

#### 6. CYCLONES

**Introduction:** Cyclones are intense circular storms originating over warm ocean waters, characterized by strong winds, heavy rains, and storm surges.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Circular cloud bands
- Calm central eye
- High wind speeds
- Heavy rainfall
- Storm surge at coast

**Formation Conditions:**
- Sea surface temperature >26-27°C
- Presence of Coriolis force
- Low vertical wind shear
- Pre-existing weather disturbance
- High humidity in lower to mid-troposphere

**Types:**

| Type | Region |
|------|--------|
| Cyclone | Indian Ocean |
| Hurricane | Atlantic and Eastern Pacific |
| Typhoon | Western Pacific |

**Structure:**
- **Eye:** Central calm area (20-50 km)
- **Eyewall:** Strongest winds and heaviest rain around eye
- **Rainbands:** Spiral bands of clouds and rain

**Characteristics:**
| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Size (average diameter) | ~600 km |
| Duration | Days to weeks |
| Central pressure | 950 mb (can be as low as 870 mb) |
| Wind speed | 120 km/hr or greater |

**Preparedness - Do's and Don'ts:**
- Listen to weather warnings
- Evacuate if advised by authorities
- Store food and water
- Secure loose objects
- Stay away from coastal areas
- Keep emergency kit ready

**Response and Recovery:**
- Evacuation of coastal populations
- Shelter management
- Medical aid and food supply
- Restoration of power and communication
- Coastal embankment repair

**Cyclone Management in India:**
- **IMD:** Cyclone tracking and warning
- **Early Warning Systems:** Cyclone detection radars
- **Evacuation plans:** Coastal district-level plans
- **Cyclone shelters:** In vulnerable coastal areas

**Vulnerable Areas in India:**
- East Coast (Bay of Bengal) - highly prone
  - Odisha
  - West Bengal
  - Andhra Pradesh
  - Tamil Nadu
- West Coast (Arabian Sea) - occasional
  - Gujarat
  - Maharashtra

**Major Cyclones in India:**
- 1999 Odisha Super Cyclone - >10,000 fatalities
- 2020 Cyclone Amphan - Severe damage in West Bengal
- 2021 Cyclone Yaas - Affected Odisha and West Bengal

#### 7. VOLCANOES

**Introduction:** Volcanic eruptions occur when magma, gases, and ash escape from beneath the Earth's crust through vents or fissures.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Smoke/ash column from mountain
- Lava flow
- Rumbling sounds
- Earth tremors
- Sulfur smell

**Causes:**
- Movement of tectonic plates
- Subduction of one plate under another
- Magma rising to surface
- Pressure buildup in magma chamber

**Types of Eruptions:**
- Effusive (lava flows out slowly)
- Explosive (violent ejection of ash and rock)

**Effects/Impacts:**
- Lava flows destroy nearby areas
- Ash clouds affect air quality and climate
- Pyroclastic surges (hot gas and rock)
- Lahars (volcanic mudflows)
- Tsunamis (if eruption is underwater)

**Volcanoes in India:**
- **Barren Island (Andaman & Nicobar):** India's only active volcano
- **Narcondam Island:** Dormant volcano
- **Deccan Traps (Maharashtra):** Ancient volcanic formations

**Preparedness:**
- Monitor seismic activity
- Monitor geothermal changes
- Gas emission monitoring
- Satellite observation
- Evacuation plans for nearby areas

#### 8. TSUNAMI

**Introduction:** A tsunami is a series of large ocean waves caused by underwater disturbances such as earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or landslides.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Sudden sea water retreat from coast
- Rapid sea level rise
- Large waves arriving in series
- Rumbling sound from ocean

**Causes:**
- Underwater earthquakes (most common)
- Volcanic eruptions
- Underwater landslides
- Meteorite impacts

**Types based on Source Distance:**
| Type | Distance from source |
|------|---------------------|
| Local Tsunami | Within 100 km, arrives within 1 hour |
| Regional Tsunami | 100-1000 km, arrives 1-3 hours |
| Tele-tsunami (Distant) | >1000 km, arrives >3 hours |

**Characteristics:**
- Waves can travel at 500-800 km/hr in deep ocean
- Wave height increases drastically near coast (up to 30+ meters)
- Multiple waves (wave train)
- Time between waves: 10-60 minutes

**Preparedness - Do's and Don'ts:**
- Move to higher ground immediately after earthquake
- Stay away from beaches
- Listen to official warnings
- Do not go to coast to watch waves
- Evacuate coastal areas when alerted

**Tsunami Management in India:**
- **Indian Tsunami Early Warning System (ITEWS):** Established by INCOIS
- **Deep-ocean sensors:** Detect tsunamis in real-time
- **Coastal monitoring systems:** Tide gauges
- **Public awareness campaigns:** Coastal community training

**Vulnerable Areas in India:**
- Southeast coast (Tamil Nadu, Andhra)
- Andaman & Nicobar Islands
- Parts of Kerala and Odisha coast

**Major Tsunami:**
- 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami - Over 10,000 deaths in India

#### 9. AVALANCHES

**Introduction:** An avalanche is a rapid flow of snow, ice, and debris down a mountain slope.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Cracking sound in snow
- Visible snow movement
- Powder cloud ahead of avalanche
- Trees and rocks being carried

**Causes:**
- Accumulated snow becoming unstable
- Weather changes (temperature rise)
- Vibrations (earthquakes, human activity)
- Heavy snowfall on weak snowpack
- Wind loading of snow

**Types:**
- **Slab avalanche:** Large cohesive plate of snow slides
- **Loose snow avalanche:** Starts from a point and widens
- **Powder avalanche:** Entirely airborne snow dust

**Vulnerable Areas in India:**
- Himalayan region
- Jammu & Kashmir (Siachen)
- Himachal Pradesh
- Uttarakhand

**Preparedness:**
- Avalanche hazard mapping
- Controlled explosions to trigger small avalanches
- Snow fences to stabilize snow
- Avoid travel in avalanche-prone areas
- Carry avalanche transceiver equipment

**Mitigation:**
- Slope monitoring
- Weather monitoring
- Warning systems for mountaineers and soldiers
- Vegetation preservation on slopes

#### 10. GLOBAL CLIMATE EXTREMES

**Introduction:** Global climate change refers to long-term alterations in temperature, precipitation, and weather patterns due to greenhouse gas emissions, causing extreme weather events.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Unusual temperature patterns
- Erratic rainfall
- Increased frequency of extreme events
- Sea level rise
- Glacier melting

**Types of Extremes:**
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Heat waves | Prolonged high temperatures |
| Cold waves | Sudden temperature drops |
| Extreme rainfall | Very heavy precipitation events |
| Cloudbursts | Intense rainfall in short duration |
| Hailstorms | Ice precipitation |
| Thunderstorms | Lightning and strong winds |
| Dust storms | High winds carrying dust |
| Sea level rise | Rising ocean levels |

**Causes:**
- Greenhouse gas emissions
- Deforestation
- Burning of fossil fuels
- Industrial activities
- Land use changes

**Effects:**
- Glacier melting (Himalayan glaciers)
- Sea level rise (coastal erosion, Sundarbans displacement)
- Extreme heat and floods
- Agricultural disruption
- Ecosystem damage

**Greenhouse Gas Scenario in India:**
- India ranks 3rd in GHG emissions
- Emissions increased by 335% since 1990
- Energy use dominated by fossil fuels (coal for electricity, petrol/diesel for transport)
- Per capita CO2 emissions still below world average

**Management in India:**
- Renewable energy expansion
- Energy efficiency programs
- Afforestation projects
- Climate-resilient infrastructure
- Adaptation strategies for vulnerable communities

### MAN-MADE DISASTERS

#### 1. TERRORISM

**Introduction:** Terrorism involves deliberate acts of violence aimed at creating fear or achieving political, religious, or ideological objectives.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Explosions in public places
- Shootings
- Hijackings
- Sabotage of infrastructure
- Hostage situations

**Types:**
- Bombings (targeting public places, transport)
- Armed attacks
- Hijackings (aircraft, vehicles)
- Cyber terrorism
- Bioterrorism

**Effects:**
- Loss of life and property
- Psychological trauma
- Economic disruption
- Social unrest
- Infrastructure damage

**Counterterrorism Measures in India:**
- Intelligence coordination (RAW, IB)
- Surveillance and monitoring
- NSG (National Security Guard) for response
- Anti-terrorism laws
- Public awareness and alertness

**Prevention Approach (Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam):**

- Value-based education
- Employment opportunities
- Spiritual transformation of religion
- Development to eliminate hunger and poverty

#### 2. GAS LEAKS

**Introduction:** Gas leaks are unintended releases of hazardous gases from industrial facilities, pipelines, or storage units that can cause poisoning, asphyxiation, or explosions.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Unusual smell (for odorized gases)
- Breathing difficulty
- Dizziness or nausea
- Hissing sound from pipelines
- Dead vegetation around leak area

**Types:**
| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| Toxic gas leak | Methyl isocyanate, chlorine, ammonia |
| Flammable gas leak | LPG, natural gas |
| Asphyxiant gas leak | Carbon monoxide, nitrogen |

**Effects:**
- Respiratory problems
- Eye irritation/blindness
- Organ damage
- Death
- Environmental contamination

**Preparedness:**
- Gas detection sensors
- Regular safety audits
- Emergency response plans
- Personal protective equipment
- Evacuation procedures

**Response:**
- Immediate evacuation of affected area
- Medical treatment for affected persons
- Containment of leak
- Neutralization of toxic effects
- Environmental monitoring

**Major Examples:**
- 1984 Bhopal Gas Tragedy - Methyl isocyanate leak, 15,000-20,000 deaths
- 2020 Visakhapatnam LG Polymers Gas Leak - Styrene gas release

#### 3. RADIATION LEAKS

**Introduction:** Radiation leaks involve the release of radioactive materials from nuclear reactors, medical facilities, or industrial sources, causing health and environmental hazards.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Detected by radiation monitoring instruments
- Unexplained health issues (nausea, burns)
- Contamination of environment
- Alarm from radiation detectors

**Types:**
- Nuclear reactor accidents
- Medical radiation accidents
- Industrial radiography accidents
- Transport accidents involving radioactive materials
- Improper disposal of radioactive waste

**Effects:**
- Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS)
- Cancer (especially thyroid)
- Genetic mutations
- Environmental contamination
- Long-term health effects

**Preparedness:**
- Multiple safety barriers
- Continuous radiation monitoring
- Emergency containment systems
- Trained response teams
- Public awareness about radiation safety

**Response:**
- Immediate evacuation of affected area
- Decontamination of affected persons
- Thyroid protection (potassium iodide tablets)
- Exclusion zone establishment
- Long-term health monitoring

**Radiation Measurement:**
- **Seivert (Sv):** Unit of radiation dose
- **Becquerel (Bq):** Unit of radioactivity

**Major Examples:**
- 1986 Chernobyl Accident (USSR) - Level 7, 350,000 evacuated
- 2011 Fukushima Daiichi (Japan) - Level 7, 100,000 evacuated
- 1993 Narora Atomic Power Station Fire (India) - Reactor safely shut down

**Nuclear Event Scale (INES):**
| Level | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| 7 | Major accident | Chernobyl, Fukushima |
| 6 | Serious accident | Kyshtym (1957) |
| 5 | Accident with wider consequences | Three Mile Island |
| 4 | Accident with local consequences | - |

#### 4. TOXIC WASTE DISPOSAL

**Introduction:** Toxic waste disposal refers to the improper handling, storage, or dumping of hazardous waste materials that can contaminate soil, water, and air.

**Key Identification Features:**
- Unusual odors from disposal sites
- Contaminated water sources
- Dead fish/vegetation near disposal areas
- Health issues in nearby communities
- Illegal dumping sites

**Types of Earthquakes:**

**Richter Scale Interpretation:**

**Causes of Earthquakes (Detailed):**

**Primary Cause:** Movement of tectonic plates creating stress that exceeds rock strength, causing sudden slip along faults.

**Process:**
1. Tectonic plates move continuously (2-15 cm/year)
2. Friction at plate boundaries prevents smooth movement
3. Stress builds up over time in rocks
4. When stress exceeds rock strength, sudden slip occurs
5. Released energy travels as seismic waves
6. Ground shaking = earthquake

**Other Causes:**
- Volcanic activity (magma movement)
- Human activities (mining, reservoir-induced seismicity, fracking)
- Collapse of underground cavities
- Nuclear explosions

| Radon gas emission | Increased radon in groundwater |
| Seismic wave velocity change | P-wave to S-wave ratio changes |

### FLOODS (Detailed Study)

**Causes of Floods (Detailed):**

**Natural Causes:**
| Cause | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Heavy rainfall | Prolonged or intense rain exceeding absorption capacity |
| Cloudburst | Extremely intense rainfall (>100 mm/hour) over small area |
| Snowmelt | Rapid melting of snow due to temperature rise |
| Cyclones/Storm surge | Sea water pushed inland by strong winds |
| Landslide/River blockage | Debris blocking river flow, causing upstream flooding |
| High tides/Sea level rise | Coastal flooding during high tides |

**Human Causes:**
| Cause | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Deforestation | Reduced water absorption, increased runoff |
| Urbanization | Impervious surfaces (concrete, asphalt) increase runoff |
| Poor drainage | Inadequate or blocked drainage systems |
| Encroachment | Building on floodplains and natural drainage channels |
| Dam mismanagement | Sudden water release without warning |
| Siltation | Reduced river capacity due to sediment deposition |

**Types of Floods (Detailed):**

**1. River Flood:**
- Occurs when river water level exceeds bank capacity
- Develops slowly (days) or rapidly depending on basin
- Covers large floodplain areas
- Example: Ganga-Brahmaputra basin floods

**2. Flash Flood:**
- Sudden flooding within minutes to 6 hours of heavy rain
- High velocity, carries debris
- Short duration (hours)
- Common in hilly areas, urban centers
- Example: Cloudburst in Uttarakhand

**3. Coastal Flood:**
- Caused by storm surges, tsunamis, or high tides
- Affects coastal low-lying areas
- Can combine with river flood (compound flooding)
- Example: Cyclone-induced coastal flooding in Odisha

**4. Urban Flood:**
- Waterlogging in cities due to poor drainage
- Exacerbated by impervious surfaces
- Occurs even with moderate rainfall
- Example: Mumbai, Chennai, Bengaluru floods

**5. Dam Breach Flood:**
- Sudden release of reservoir water due to dam failure
- Extremely destructive, fast-moving wave
- Catastrophic downstream impact
- Example: Machhu Dam failure (Gujarat, 1979)

**Flood Frequency and Return Period:**
- **100-year flood:** Flood with 1% probability of occurring in any given year
- **Return period:** Average time between floods of given magnitude
- **Discharge (Q):** Volume of water flowing per second (cumecs = m³/s)

**Flood Estimation (Rational Formula for Urban Areas):**
```
Q = C × I × A
Where:
Q = Peak runoff rate (cumecs)
C = Runoff coefficient (0.3 for barren land, 0.7-0.9 for urban)
I = Rainfall intensity (mm/hr)
A = Catchment area (km²)
```

### LANDSLIDES (Detailed Study)

**Causes of Landslides (Detailed):**

**Natural Causes:**
| Cause | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| Heavy rainfall | Increases pore water pressure, adds weight, reduces friction |
| Earthquakes | Ground shaking destabilizes slopes |
| Volcanic eruption | Ash deposition adds load, seismic activity |
| Weathering | Weakens rock over time |
| Erosion | River/wave action undercuts slope toe |
| Freeze-thaw | Water in cracks freezes, expands, breaks rock |

**Anthropogenic (Human) Causes:**
| Cause | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| Deforestation | Loss of root cohesion, increased runoff |
| Mining/Quarrying | Slope undercutting, blast vibrations |
| Road construction | Slope cutting, drainage alteration |
| Building construction | Added load on slope |
| Agricultural practices | Terrace failure, irrigation water infiltration |
| Reservoir drawdown | Sudden water level drop reduces slope support |

**Types of Landslides (Detailed with Characteristics):**

**1. Falls:**
- Free fall of rock/soil from steep cliff
- Very fast (high velocity)
- No defined slip surface
- Example: Rockfall in Himalayas

**2. Topples:**
- Forward rotation around pivot point
- Columnar rock blocks tilting outward
- Common in steep slopes with vertical joints
- Can transform into falls or flows

**3. Slides:**
- Movement along defined slip surface

**a) Rotational Slide (Slump):**
- Curved, concave slip surface (spoon-shaped)
- Backward tilting of upper mass
- Hummocky toe (lumpy mound)
- Common in clay-rich slopes, homogeneous materials
- Often slow to moderate speed

**b) Translational Slide:**
- Planar/flat slip surface
- Little to no rotation
- Moves as coherent block
- Often along bedding planes, joints, or weak layers
- Can be very large and destructive

**4. Flows:**
- Fluid-like movement, no defined slip surface

**a) Debris Flow:**
- Mixture of soil, rock, water (slurry)
- Fast (up to 35 mph or more)
- Carries large objects (boulders, trees)
- Common in steep drainages after heavy rain

**b) Mud Flow:**
- >50% silt and clay particles
- More fluid than debris flow
- Can travel long distances
- Often follows channels

**c) Rock Flow/Rock Avalanche:**
- Very rapid flow of fragmented rock
- Extremely destructive
- Can travel kilometers

**5. Creep:**
- Extremely slow movement (mm/year)
- Continuous over long periods
- Signs: bent trees ("pistol butt"), tilted fences
- Caused by wetting/drying, freeze-thaw cycles

**6. Lateral Spread:**
- Sideways movement of intact blocks
- Occurs on gentle slopes
- Caused by liquefaction of underlying layer
- Triggered by earthquakes

|--------|------------|--------|
| North-Western Himalayas | 66.5% | J&K, Himachal, Uttarakhand |

| Western Ghats | 14.7% | Maharashtra, Goa, Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu |
| Eastern Ghats | Minor | Andhra Pradesh (Araku region) |

- 12.6% of India's geographical area prone to landslides
- Northeastern region comprises ~50% of total prone area

**Before Landslide (Do's):**
- Identify landslide-prone areas in your region
- Plant trees on upper slopes (afforestation)

- Ensure proper drainage to prevent water saturation
- Avoid construction on steep slopes (>15%)

**Before Landslide (Don'ts):**
- Don't block natural water drainage channels
- Don't cut slopes without proper engineering
- Don't remove vegetation on slopes
- Don't build on or near active landslide zones

**During Landslide (Do's):**
- Listen for unusual sounds (cracking, rumbling, falling trees)
- Watch for sudden changes in water flow

- If inside, take cover under sturdy furniture
- If outside, run perpendicular to slide direction
- Protect head from debris

**During Landslide (Don'ts):**
- Don't stay in path of moving debris
- Don't cross landslide-damaged roads
- Don't return to slide area until cleared

**After Landslide (Do's):**
- Stay away from slide area (secondary slides possible)

**After Landslide (Don'ts):**

### CYCLONES (Detailed Study)

**Causes of Cyclones:**

**Required Conditions for Formation:**
1. Sea surface temperature >26.5°C (up to 60m depth)
2. Coriolis force sufficient (at least 5° from equator)
3. Low vertical wind shear (<10 m/s)
4. Pre-existing disturbance (low pressure system)
5. High humidity in lower and mid-troposphere
6. Unstable atmosphere

**Formation Process:**
1. Warm ocean water evaporates, rises
2. Rising air creates low pressure area
3. Surrounding air rushes in (Coriolis force causes rotation)
4. Rising air cools, condenses, releases latent heat
5. Heat release intensifies low pressure
6. Cycle continues, storm strengthens
7. Wind speed increases, organized structure develops

**Classification (Indian Meteorological Department):**

| Category | Wind Speed (km/h) | Terminology |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| <31 | - | Low pressure area |
| 31-50 | - | Depression |
| 51-62 | - | Deep depression |
| 63-88 | - | Cyclonic storm |
| 89-118 | - | Severe cyclonic storm |
| 119-165 | - | Very severe cyclonic storm |
| 166-221 | - | Extremely severe cyclonic storm |
| >222 | - | Super cyclonic storm |

**Structure of a Cyclone:**
- **Eye:** Central calm area (20-50 km diameter), clear skies, light winds
- **Eyewall:** Rings of intense thunderstorms around eye, strongest winds, heaviest rain
- **Rainbands:** Spiral bands extending outward, heavy rain, moderate winds

**Cyclone Naming in Indian Ocean Region:**
- Eight countries contribute names (India, Bangladesh, Maldives, Myanmar, Oman, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, Thailand)
- India's recent names: Gati, Nivar, Burevi, Tauktae, Yaas, Asani, Sitrang

### DROUGHTS (Detailed Study)

**Causes of Droughts (Detailed):**

**Meteorological Causes:**
| Cause | Description |

**Hydrological Causes:**

**Agricultural Causes:**
- Soil moisture deficit

**Socio-economic Causes:**
- Overexploitation of water resources
- Inefficient irrigation practices
- Water-intensive crops in dry areas
- Poor water management policies
- Population growth increasing demand

**Types of Droughts (Detailed):**

**1. Meteorological Drought:**
- Definition: Rainfall significantly below normal for region
- Onset: First stage of drought
- Measurement: Percentage departure from normal rainfall
- Classification (IMD):
  - Slight: 11-25% below normal
  - Moderate: 26-50% below normal
  - Severe: >50% below normal
- Other types: Seasonal, contingent, permanent, atmospheric, soil drought

**2. Hydrological Drought:**
- Definition: Reduced water levels in rivers, lakes, reservoirs, groundwater
- Onset: Lags behind meteorological drought (weeks to months)
- Duration: Can continue after rainfall returns (recharge time)
- Types: Surface water drought, groundwater drought
- Indicators: Streamflow deficit, reservoir level drop, groundwater level decline

**3. Agricultural Drought:**
- Definition: Soil moisture insufficient for crop growth
- Onset: Based on crop water requirements
- Factors: Rainfall, soil type, crop stage, temperature
- Types:
  - Early-season drought: Delayed sowing, seedling mortality
  - Mid-season drought: Between rainfall events during growth
  - Late-season drought: Early withdrawal, affects reproduction
  - Permanent drought: Arid regions, no agriculture without irrigation
  - Invisible drought: Daily rain insufficient for plant needs

**4. Socio-economic Drought:**
- Definition: Water shortage affects human activities, economy
- Onset: When demand exceeds supply
- Indicators: Food prices, employment, migration, health
- Impacts: Economic losses, food insecurity, social disruption

**Drought Indicators and Indices:**

| Index | Description | Use |
|-------|-------------|-----|
| SPI (Standardized Precipitation Index) | Measures rainfall probability | Early warning, severity |
| PDSI (Palmer Drought Severity Index) | Soil moisture, temperature, precipitation | Agricultural impact |
| Rainfall Deviation | Percentage departure from normal | Meteorological drought |
| NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) | Satellite-based vegetation health | Agricultural drought |
| Groundwater level | Depth to water table | Hydrological drought |

### TSUNAMI (Detailed Study)

**Causes of Tsunami:**

**Primary Causes:**
| Cause | Mechanism | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Underwater earthquake (most common) | Vertical displacement of seafloor | 80% of tsunamis |
| Underwater volcanic eruption | Explosive displacement, caldera collapse | Less common |
| Underwater landslide | Submarine slope failure | Localized |
| Meteorite impact | Very rare, extremely large waves | Extremely rare |

**Tsunami Generation Mechanism:**
1. Earthquake occurs at subduction zone
2. Seafloor suddenly moves upward or downward
3. Overlying water column is displaced
4. Displaced water forms waves
5. Waves radiate outward in all directions
6. In deep ocean: long wavelength (100s km), small height (<1m)
7. Near coast: wave slows, height increases dramatically (up to 30m)

**Types of Tsunami (by Distance):**

| Type | Distance from Source | Travel Time | Warning Time |
|------|---------------------|-------------|--------------|
| Local Tsunami | <100 km | <1 hour | Very short (minutes) |
| Regional Tsunami | 100-1000 km | 1-3 hours | Short |
| Distant (Tele-tsunami) | >1000 km | >3 hours | Moderate |

**Characteristics:**
- Wave speed in deep ocean: 500-800 km/h (jet speed)
- Wavelength: 100-500 km
- Wave period: 10-60 minutes between crests
- Multiple waves (wave train; first wave not always largest)
- Height increases near coast due to shoaling

**Preparedness (Do's and Don'ts):**

**Before Tsunami (Do's):**
- Know tsunami-prone areas near coast
- Learn natural warning signs: strong earthquake, sudden sea water retreat
- Know evacuation routes to higher ground
- Prepare emergency kit
- Participate in tsunami drills

**Before Tsunami (Don'ts):**
- Don't live in low-lying coastal areas if possible
- Don't ignore earthquake shaking (possible tsunami warning)

**During Tsunami (Do's):**
- Move to higher ground immediately after strong earthquake
- Go at least 30 meters above sea level or 3 km inland
- Follow official evacuation orders
- If no warning, but see sea water retreating, run to high ground
- Listen to official alerts via radio

**During Tsunami (Don'ts):**
- Don't go to coast to watch waves
- Don't wait for official warning if you feel strong earthquake
- Don't use car (roads may be blocked); run on foot
- Don't return to coast until officially cleared

**After Tsunami (Do's):**
- Stay away from damaged areas
- Help injured and trapped persons
- Listen to official updates
- Beware of aftershocks and secondary tsunamis
- Check for structural damage before entering buildings
- Drink boiled water (contamination risk)

**After Tsunami (Don'ts):**
- Don't assume one wave is end (multiple waves arrive)
- Don't sightsee in affected areas
- Don't spread rumors

**Response and Recovery:**
- Immediate search and rescue
- Medical aid for injured
- Mass casualty management
- Temporary shelters for displaced
- Disease outbreak prevention
- Debris clearance
- Infrastructure restoration
- Psycho-social support
- Livelihood restoration (fishing communities)

**Mitigation Measures:**
| Measure | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Early warning systems | Deep-ocean sensors, tide gauges, seismic networks |
| Coastal zoning | Restrict construction in high-risk zones |
| Evacuation routes | Marked paths to high ground |
| Vertical evacuation structures | Multi-story buildings on coast |
| Mangrove preservation | Natural buffer against waves |
| Public awareness | Education on natural warning signs |
| Building codes | Tsunami-resistant construction |

**Tsunami Management in India:**

**Indian Tsunami Early Warning System (ITEWES) at INCOIS, Hyderabad:**
| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Seismic network | Real-time earthquake detection (Indian Ocean) |
| Bottom Pressure Recorders (BPR) | Deep-ocean sensors to detect tsunamis |
| Tide gauges | Coastal sea level monitoring |
| Numerical models | Forecast wave arrival time and height |
| 24×7 Warning Centre | Continuous monitoring, alert dissemination |

**Vulnerable Areas in India:**
- Tamil Nadu coast (Chennai, Nagapattinam, Cuddalore, Kanyakumari)
- Andhra Pradesh coast
- Andaman & Nicobar Islands (epicenter proximity)

- Don't ignore gas leak smell (don't use switches, just open windows and leave)

**During Fire Do's:**
- Shout "Fire, Fire, Fire" to alert others
- Use fire extinguisher if small and trained
- Crawl low under smoke (smoke rises)
- Cover nose and mouth with wet cloth
- Feel doors before opening (if hot, don't open)
- Use stairs, never elevators
- Stop, drop, and roll if clothes catch fire

**During Fire Don'ts:**
- Don't waste time collecting belongings
- Don't re-enter burning building
- Don't use elevators
- Don't open hot doors
- Don't hide in cupboards or under beds
- Don't jump from heights (wait for rescue)

**After Fire Do's:**
- Meet at designated assembly point
- Report to roll call
- Do not re-enter until cleared
- Seek medical attention for smoke inhalation
- Report to authorities

## UNIT 3: MITIGATION AND MANAGEMENT TECHNIQUES OF DISASTER

### BASIC PRINCIPLES OF DISASTER MANAGEMENT

**Definition:** Disaster management involves the organized efforts to mitigate, prepare for, respond to, and recover from disasters, with the primary goal of reducing negative impacts on human life, property, and environment.

**Aim and Objectives of Disaster Management:**

1. **Prevent or Reduce Risk of Disasters:**
   - Identify potential hazards and vulnerabilities
   - Implement preventive measures (building regulations, environmental management, land-use planning)

2. **Prepare for Disasters:**
   - Develop emergency plans
   - Conduct training and drills
   - Establish early warning systems

3. **Effective Response During Disasters:**
   - Coordinate emergency response among agencies
   - Provide immediate assistance (medical care, shelter, food)
   - Ensure efficient communication

4. **Recovery and Rehabilitation:**
   - Restore essential services and infrastructure
   - Provide psychological support
   - Implement long-term reconstruction programs

**Significance of Disaster Management:**
- **Saving lives and reducing suffering:** Reduces fatalities, provides timely support
- **Economic stability:** Minimizes damage to infrastructure and business
- **Environmental protection:** Prevents further degradation, promotes sustainability
- **Community resilience:** Enhances ability to withstand and recover
- **Resource optimization:** Efficient use of resources during all phases

---

### DISASTER CONCEPTS AND FORMULAS

**Disaster Risk Formula:**
```
Disaster Risk = (Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability) / Capacity
```

**Risk = Probability × Impact**

**Components of Risk:**

| Component | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| Hazard | Potentially damaging physical event |
| Exposure | People, assets present in hazard-prone areas |
| Vulnerability | Susceptibility to damage |
| Capacity | Ability to cope, resist, recover |

**Progression of Vulnerability:**
```
Root Causes → Dynamic Pressures → Unsafe Conditions → Disaster
```

**Root Causes:** Limited access to resources, power structures, economic systems
**Dynamic Pressures:** Lack of training, rapid urbanization, population growth
**Unsafe Conditions:** Fragile buildings, lack of warning systems, environmental degradation

**Risk Reduction Formula:**
```
Risk Reduction = Hazard Reduction + Vulnerability Reduction + Exposure Reduction + Capacity Enhancement
```

**Risk Mapping:** Process of identifying and visualizing areas with different risk levels using GIS and spatial data.

**Risk Mapping Steps:**
1. Hazard mapping (where hazards can occur)
2. Vulnerability assessment (who/what is vulnerable)
3. Exposure analysis (population, assets in hazard zones)
4. Risk calculation and zoning
5. Map production

**Disaster Levels (Scale):**

| Level | Name | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 0 | Below scale | Normal, no safety significance, no hazard/damage |
| 1 | Anomaly | Small abnormal condition, no injury, warning signal |
| 2 | Incident | Minor event, easily controllable, no serious injury |
| 3 | Near-miss | Could have caused harm but did not, indicates unsafe system |
| 4 | Accident with local consequences | Damage limited to small area |
| 5 | - | (Not specified) |
| 6 | Serious accident | Major injuries, emergency response needed |
| 7 | Major disaster | Catastrophic impact |

**Key Principle:** "Disasters are not sudden; they are the result of a chain of ignored incidents and anomalies."

### DISASTER MANAGEMENT CYCLE

**Five Phases of Disaster Management:**

```
           PREVENTION
               ↑
               ↓
           MITIGATION
               ↑
               ↓
          PREPAREDNESS
               ↑
               ↓
             RESPONSE
               ↑
               ↓
            RECOVERY
```

**1. PREVENTION:**
- **Definition:** Proactive approach to avoid disasters by identifying hazards and devising safeguards.
- **Examples:** 
  - Evacuation plans in schools
  - City planning with flood mitigation (locks, dams, channels)
  - Building codes to prevent collapse
- **Timing:** Long-term, ongoing

**2. MITIGATION:**
- **Definition:** Action to reduce the impact of a disaster and minimize loss of human life.
- **Types:**
  | Type | Description | Example |
  |------|-------------|---------|
  | Structural | Physical changes to environment | Clearing trees near houses, building flood walls |
  | Non-structural | Policies, codes, regulations | Building code amendments, zoning laws |
- **Timing:** Before disaster (long-term)

**3. PREPAREDNESS:**
- **Definition:** Ongoing process of planning and training for what to do in a disaster.
- **Activities:**
  - Fire drills, active-shooter drills
  - Evacuation rehearsals
  - Emergency kit preparation
  - Public awareness campaigns
  - Early warning system testing
- **Timing:** Before disaster (ongoing)

**4. RESPONSE:**
- **Definition:** Actions taken during and immediately after a disaster.
- **Activities (Short-term):**
  - Search and rescue
  - Medical aid
  - Firefighting
  - Shelter provision
  - Food and water distribution
  - Hazard removal (e.g., putting out lingering fires)
- **Activities (Long-term - weeks to months):**
  - Disease control
  - Temporary housing
  - Restoration of essential services
- **Timing:** During and immediately after disaster

**5. RECOVERY:**
- **Definition:** Returning to normalcy after a disaster.
- **Phases of Recovery:**
  | Phase | Timeframe | Activities |
  |-------|-----------|-------------|
  | Emergency Rescue | 1-2 weeks | Life rescue, public kitchen, temporary settlement |
  | Rehabilitation | 2-12 months | Restore basic services, recover economic facilities, mental rehabilitation |
  | Reconstruction | 7-30 months | Rebuild infrastructure, transportation, telecommunication, social and heritage structures |

**Stages of Disaster Relief (Timeline):**
- **1st weeks:** Health needs change from casualty treatment to primary health care
- **Later weeks:** Shift from health care to environmental health measures

### ANALYSIS VS ASSESSMENT

| Aspect | Analysis | Assessment |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Focus | Understanding the phenomenon | Evaluating consequences |
| Nature | Scientific & technical | Decision-oriented |
| Question | What is happening and why? | So what should we do? |
| Stage | Pre-disaster / During | Pre, During & Post-disaster |
| Output | Data, models, trends | Risk levels, action plans |
| Precision | More precise | General (comparison) |

**Analysis involves:** Deep scrutiny by measurement, recording, calculation
**Assessment involves:** Comparison to a standard or between scenarios

### DISASTER PARADIGM

**Definition:** The disaster paradigm explains how a normal hazardous event turns into a disaster due to human vulnerability, exposure, and lack of preparedness.

**Simple Formulation:**
```
A hazard becomes a disaster only when it affects vulnerable people and systems that cannot cope.
```

**Paradigm Explanation:**
- **Hazard alone ≠ Disaster**
- **Hazard + Vulnerability + Exposure = Disaster** (when capacity insufficient)

**Example:**
- Earthquake in uninhabited area → Hazard (not disaster)
- Earthquake in densely populated area with weak buildings → Disaster

**Incident Escalation to Disaster:**
```
Initiating Incident → Hazardous Environment → Exposure → Vulnerability → Barrier Failure → Escalation → Disaster
```

**Example:**
```
Small chemical leak (initiating) → In a chemical plant (hazardous environment) → Workers exposed → No PPE (vulnerability) → Safety valve fails (barrier failure) → Large explosion (escalation) → Major disaster
```

### CORDON OFF ZONES

**Definition:** Cordon off zones are designated, controlled perimeters established to restrict unauthorized access, protect the public from hazards, and facilitate the work of emergency responders.

**Significance:**
- Establishes safety and security at incident scene
- Prevents unauthorized entry into dangerous areas
- Protects public from hazards (debris, toxic materials, fire)
- Enables emergency responders to work without interference
- Creates organized management of affected area

**Cordon Levels (Gold-Silver-Bronze Command Structure):**

**Gold Zone - Strategic Command:**
| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Level | Highest (strategic) |
| Location | Away from disaster (headquarters) |
| Responsibility | Overall strategy, policy, resource allocation, inter-agency coordination |
| Decisions | Evacuation, funding, public communication, external assistance |
| Example (India) | NDMA/SDMA during Uttarakhand floods 2013 |

**Silver Zone - Tactical Command:**
| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Level | Middle (tactical) |
| Location | Close to disaster area (safe zone) |
| Responsibility | Translate strategy into operational plans, coordinate agencies |
| Decisions | Logistics, resource deployment, inter-agency coordination |
| Example (India) | District collector during chemical plant explosion |

**Bronze Zone - Operational Command:**
| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Level | Frontline (operational) |
| Location | Within or near affected area |
| Responsibility | Execute field operations (rescue, firefighting, medical aid) |
| Actions | Immediate life-saving, reporting to Silver |
| Example (India) | NDRF teams, firefighters at collapsed building |

**Establishment of Cordon Zones:**
1. **Assessment:** Authorities assess extent of disaster, identify areas needing cordon
2. **Perimeter Definition:** Boundaries clearly marked with tape, barriers, or personnel
3. **Access Control:** Entry/exit points established, access restricted to authorized personnel
4. **Communication:** Clear signage and communication systems inform public

**In Indian Context (Mumbai example):**
- **SDMA** (State Disaster Management Authority) responsible for disaster management plans including cordons
- **DDMP** (District Disaster Management Plan) outlines procedures for cordoning areas
- **NDRF** involved in setting up cordons and managing affected areas

### EARLY WARNING SYSTEMS (EWS)

**Definition:** Early warning systems provide timely information to at-risk populations, authorities, and responders to prepare for and respond to potential hazards.

**Need for EWS:**
- Critical for efficient short-term preparation and response
- Contributes to increasing resilience
- Enhances ability to recover rapidly
- Minimizes vulnerability
- Saves lives and reduces property damage

**The Four Levels of Early Warning System:**

```
Level 1: Risk Knowledge
     ↓
Level 2: Monitoring & Forecasting
     ↓
Level 3: Warning Dissemination
     ↓
Level 4: Response Capability
```

**Level 1: Risk Knowledge**
- **Purpose:** Identify hazards, vulnerable areas, and risk levels
- **Activities:**
  - Hazard monitoring (satellites, radar, seismometers)
  - Vulnerability assessment (population density, land use, building codes)
  - Risk analysis (likelihood and potential impact)
  - Data management (collection, storage, analysis)

**Level 2: Monitoring and Evaluation**
- **Purpose:** Track hazards in real-time, forecast events
- **Activities:**
  - Performance monitoring (warning lead times, forecast accuracy)
  - Impact assessment (post-event evaluation)
  - Feedback mechanisms from end-users
  - Learning and innovation (continuous improvement)

**Level 3: Warning Dissemination and Communication**
- **Purpose:** Send timely, accurate warnings to at-risk people
- **Activities:**
  - Warning systems (sirens, radio, TV, SMS, mobile apps, social media)
  - Multi-hazard approach (integrate various threats)
  - Risk communication (clear, concise, culturally appropriate)
  - Community engagement (involve communities in design)

**Level 4: Response Capability and Preparedness**
- **Purpose:** Strengthen capacity to respond effectively
- **Activities:**
  - Emergency planning (comprehensive response plans)
  - Training and capacity building (for responders, volunteers)
  - Resource mobilization (supplies, equipment, shelters)
  - Drills and exercises (table-top, full-scale simulations)

**Types of Early Warning Systems:**

**By Type of Hazard:**
- Geological hazards (tsunamis, earthquakes, volcanoes, landslides)
- Hydro-meteorological hazards (floods, droughts, cyclones, heat/cold waves)
- Forest fires
- Biological hazards (locusts, algae blooms)
- Health hazards (diseases, epidemics)
- Agricultural pests and diseases

**By Level of Operation:**
| Level | Operator | Example |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Community | Local government, community | Local flood warning |
| National | National agency (IMD, GSI) | Cyclone warnings |
| Regional | Multi-country | FEWSNET, MeteoAlarm |
| Global | International (WHO, FAO) | Pandemic alerts |

**By Number of Hazards:**
- **Single-hazard:** Community systems (usually one hazard)
- **Multi-hazard:** Meteorological departments, health ministries (multiple hazards)

**Modes of Early Warning Systems:**

**1. Mass Notification Systems:**
- Platform for sending important messages to large numbers of people
- Primary aim: Deliver emergency communication during crises
- Method: Recorded calls, emails, app notifications

**2. Wireless Emergency Alerts (WEA):**
- Free, 360-character messages from authorized government agencies
- Sent via cell towers to mobile devices
- Targeted, real-time alerts for imminent threats
- In US: FEMA's IPAWS system
- In India: Common alerting protocol-based alerts

**3. SMS Text Systems:**
- Near real-time targeted alerts
- Cell Broadcast (CB) - sends to all phones in cell area
- API-based bulk SMS - to registered users
- 98% read rates for instant communication
- Example: SACHET system for cyclone alerts

**4. Outdoor Public Warning Systems (OPWS):**
- Sirens, loudspeakers, digital signage
- 24/7 infrastructure for immediate alerting
- Triggers rapid evacuation or protective action
- Enhances community preparedness

**Gas Leak / Radiation / Global Extremes Checklist:**

**Gas Leak Response Checklist:**
- [ ] Do not use any electrical switches
- [ ] Do not light matches or candles
- [ ] Open windows and doors (if safe)
- [ ] Turn off gas cylinder valve
- [ ] Evacuate building immediately
- [ ] Move upwind of leak

- [ ] Close all windows and doors

- [ ] Shower with soap and water

## 1. The Panshet Dam Bursts Floods (1961)

## 2. Cyclone Nisarga (2020)

**Introduction:** Cyclone Nisarga was a severe cyclonic storm that struck Maharashtra coast on 3 June 2020, becoming the first cyclone to hit Mumbai since 1948.

**Causes:**
- Warm sea surface temperatures (30-31°C) in Arabian Sea
- Low vertical wind shear and high moisture
- Climate change-induced increase in Arabian Sea cyclone frequency

**What Happened:**
- Landfall near Alibag (Raigad district), 100 km south of Mumbai

- Storm surge: 1-2 meters in coastal areas
- **Impacts:**
  - 6 deaths (4 in Maharashtra, 2 in Gujarat)
  - Over 9,000 people evacuated
  - Power lines downed; trees uprooted (Mumbai – 400+ trees)

**Before the Cyclone (Preparedness):**
- IMD issued warnings 72 hours in advance (cyclone alert)

- NDRF pre-positioned 14 teams (16 more on standby)

- Fishermen warned not to venture into sea

**Lessons Learned:**
- Arabian Sea cyclones are increasing; need year-round preparedness
- COVID + cyclone requires dual disaster management plans
- Timely evacuation saves lives even with high population density
- Power backup critical for hospitals during cyclones

**Do’s & Don’ts (Specific to Nisarga):**
- Do not ignore cyclone warnings even if you live in a city
- Do keep emergency kit even during pandemic
- Don't venture to seafront (e.g., Marine Drive, Gateway) during cyclone

## 3. Kolhapur Flood (2019)

## 4. North Indian Floods (2013 – Uttarakhand Disaster)

**Introduction:** The 2013 North Indian floods (June 16-17), centered in Uttarakhand, was India’s worst humanitarian disaster since the 2004 tsunami, caused by a cloudburst and glacial lake outburst flood (GLOF) that devastated Kedarnath valley.

**Causes:**
- **Cloudburst:** 375 mm rainfall in 24 hours (normal June rainfall for entire month is 60 mm)
- **Chorabari Lake (Kedarnath) burst:** Glacial lake overflowed due to extreme rain and melting
- **Multiple landslides:** Blocked rivers, created temporary dams that then burst
- **Pilgrim season:** 1.5 lakh people trapped in high-altitude areas (Kedarnath, Badrinath, Gangotri, Yamunotri)

**What Happened:**
- **Deaths:** 5,700+ (official), some estimates >10,000 (including missing)
- **Destruction:** Entire towns washed away – Kedarnath town flattened, Gaurikund destroyed
- **Infrastructure:** 1,200 bridges (including 14 major ones) destroyed; 4,500 km roads damaged
- **Helicopter crash:** 20+ died in rescue helicopter crash
- **Displaced:** 1 lakh+ people evacuated; 50,000+ stranded for days without food/water
- **Economic loss:** ₹4,000+ crore

**Before the Disaster:**
- IMD issued heavy rainfall warning (but did not anticipate cloudburst magnitude)
- No GLOF monitoring system in place
- No evacuation plan for pilgrims during extreme weather
- Construction in floodplains and landslide-prone areas unchecked

**During the Disaster:**
- **16 June:** Mandakini River rose 15 feet in 2 hours; Kedarnath submerged
- **Response:** Indian Army, Air Force, NDRF, ITBP, BRO (Border Roads Organisation) deployed
- **Air rescue:** 2.5 lakh people airlifted by IAF (largest helicopter rescue operation in history)
- **Foot evacuation:** Pilgrims walked 100+ km to safety over landslides
- **Challenges:** No connectivity, roads washed away, helicopters limited by weather

**After the Disaster (Relief & Rehabilitation):**
- **Temporary shelters:** Set up in Rishikesh, Haridwar, Dehradun
- **Compensation:** ₹15 lakh for deceased pilgrims, ₹5 lakh for locals
- **Announcement:** `Kedarnath Reconstruction Plan` with ₹2,000 crore allocation
- **Long-term:** Relocation of people from high-risk zones; building codes strengthened

**Government Actions Post-2013:**
- **National GLOF Program:** Glacial lake monitoring and early warning systems (SASE, WIHG)
- **Cloudburst forecasting:** Advanced weather models and Doppler radars in mountains
- **Char Dham road project:** All-weather roads with landslide mitigation
- **Ban on construction in floodplains:** Enforced in Uttarakhand hill areas
- **Early warning system:** River level sensors and sirens downstream of glacial lakes

**Lessons Learned:**
- Climate change is increasing cloudburst and GLOF frequency
- Pilgrim/tourism management during extreme weather is inadequate
- Satellite-based GLOF monitoring is essential
- Helicopter rescue capacity must be increased in mountain states

**Do’s & Don’ts:**
- Don't travel to high-altitude pilgrimage sites during extreme rainfall warnings
- Do carry emergency supplies (food, water, medicine) even on short treks
- Don't build hotels/houses in river floodplains of Himalayan rivers

## 6. Bhopal Gas Tragedy (1984)

**Introduction:** On the night of 2-3 December 1984, about 45 tons of methyl isocyanate (MIC) gas leaked from the Union Carbide India Limited (UCIL) pesticide plant in Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh, causing the world's worst industrial disaster.

**Causes (Human Negligence & Technical Failures):**
- **Water ingress:** Water entered MIC storage tank 610 (due to improper valve maintenance)
- **Exothermic reaction:** Water reacted with MIC, temperature rose to 200°C, pressure built up
- **Safety systems non-functional:**
  - Refrigeration system (should keep MIC at 0°C) – shut down for months
  - Flare tower (to burn toxic gas) – under repair
  - Vent gas scrubber (to neutralize gas) – not switched on
  - Water curtain (to absorb gas) – insufficient height
- **Slack safety culture:** Staff cut, training inadequate, emergency plan never tested

**What Happened:**
- **Timing:** Gas leaked from 12:30 AM to 2:00 AM on 3 December
- **Wind direction:** Winds carried gas toward densely populated slums (J.P. Nagar, Chhola, Kazi Camp)
- **Deaths:** Immediate ~3,000-8,000; final estimate 15,000-20,000
- **Injuries:** 5,00,000+ survivors with chronic health effects (respiratory, eye, neurological, reproductive)
- **Animal deaths:** 3,500+ cattle died
- **Environmental contamination:** Soil and groundwater near plant still contaminated (2024)

**Before the Tragedy:**
- No public warning system or emergency plan
- Plant located in densely populated area (violation of industrial siting norms)
- Workers unaware of chemical hazards
- Community near plant not educated about what to do in gas leak

**During the Tragedy:**
- **Response:** Police fled; hospital overwhelmed within hours
- **Action:** Victims ran toward plant (instead of upwind) due to confusion
- **Government reaction:** At 3 AM, few officials woke; no public announcement until morning
- **Treatment:** No antidote for MIC; doctors treated symptoms (oxygen, eye wash)

**After the Tragedy (Relief & Rehabilitation):**
- **Immediate:** Free food, water, and medical camps set up days later
- **Compensation:** ₹10,000 for deceased (later increased); final settlement of $470 million in 1989
- **Health effects:** 5,00,000+ suffer from chronic illnesses; government set up Bhopal Memorial Hospital & Research Centre
- **Waste disposal:** Toxic waste (350 tonnes) remains at plant site; finally incinerated in 2024 (40 years later!)

**Government & Legal Actions:**
- **Union Carbide:** Officers convicted (7-10 years) but served only token time; Warren Anderson (CEO) fled India
- **Environmental laws:** Environment Protection Act, 1986 passed; Hazardous Waste Rules
- **Industrial safety:** Factories Act amended; creation of Ministry of Environment & Forests
- **Bhopal Gas Tragedy Relief and Rehabilitation Department:** Set up for long-term relief

**Mitigation & Lessons Learned:**
- Industrial hazardous units must be located away from residential areas (buffer zones of 5-10 km)
- Multiple redundant safety systems required (defense in depth)
- On-site and off-site emergency plans must be tested regularly
- Community must be trained on evacuation (move upwind, cover nose with wet cloth)
- Public right to know about hazards in their area

**Do’s & Don’ts:**
- Don't site hazardous industries near dense population
- Do maintain all safety systems (refrigeration, scrubber, flare tower)
- Do conduct regular mock drills with community
- In gas leak: move crosswind/upwind, not downwind; cover nose and mouth with wet cloth

## 7. Heat Wave in India (2015)

**Causes:**
- **Meteorological:** Delayed monsoon, high pressure over Bay of Bengal blocked moisture
- **Climate change:** Increasing frequency and intensity of heat waves

**What Happened:**
- **Temperatures:** 47-48°C in Andhra and Telangana; Delhi 46°C; Odisha 45°C
- **Deaths:** 2,500+ (Andhra 1,850, Telangana 585, others 100+)

- **Power demand:** Peak load led to blackouts in many areas
- **Livestock:** Thousands of cattle and poultry died

**Before the Heat Wave:**
- IMD issued warnings (but many state governments slow to act)
- No public awareness campaigns on heat wave dangers
- No cooling centers or water distribution plans
- Outdoor work continued during peak hours (12-3 PM)

**During the Heat Wave:**
- **Government response:** Crisis meetings held after deaths mounted
- **Actions:** 
  - Water distribution points set up in cities
  - School timings changed (morning only)
  - Hospitals put on high alert for heat stroke cases
  - Workers advised to take afternoon rest
  - Shelters for homeless opened
- **Challenges:** Many rural areas had no access to warnings or help

- **Compensation:** ₹1.5 lakh per death (ex gratia)

- **Implemented in:** 100+ cities across 17 states by 2023

**Lessons Learned:**
- Heat waves kill more people in India than any other natural hazard (except floods)
- Prevention is cheap: water, shade, awareness, and rescheduling work
- Vulnerable groups: elderly, children, outdoor workers, homeless – need targeted interventions

**Do’s & Don’ts:**
- Do drink plenty of water even if not thirsty
- Do wear light-colored, loose cotton clothes

- Don't leave children/pets in parked cars
- Don't drink alcohol, tea, coffee (dehydrate)

## 8. Gujarat Earthquake (2001)

**Introduction:** On 26 January 2001 (Republic Day), a magnitude 7.7 earthquake struck Bhuj in Gujarat’s Kutch district, killing over 20,000 people and destroying more than a million houses.

**Causes:**
- **Tectonic:** Movement along hidden fault line in Kutch region (not previously considered high risk)
- **Shallow depth:** Focus at 23 km – intense ground shaking
- **Poor construction:** Most buildings not earthquake-resistant; old masonry houses collapsed

**What Happened:**
- **Magnitude:** 7.7 (Richter)
- **Epicenter:** 20 km NE of Bhuj
- **Duration:** Shaking lasted 2 minutes
- **Deaths:** 20,023 (official); injured 1,67,000
- **Houses destroyed:** 3,47,000 fully; 9,60,000 partially
- **Infrastructure damage:** Roads, bridges, power lines, water supply systems collapsed
- **Economic loss:** ₹15,000 crore (industry, agriculture, livestock – 2.5 lakh cattle died)
- **Historic destruction:** Bhuj town 90% flattened; Gandhidham, Anjar, Rapar severely hit
- **Schools collapsed:** Hundreds of children died (worst in Anjar)

**Before the Earthquake:**
- No early warning possible (earthquakes cannot be predicted)
- Building codes not enforced in Kutch
- Public unaware of earthquake safety

**During the Earthquake:**
- **Panic:** People ran out of buildings; many crushed by falling debris
- **No communication:** Phone lines down; Bhuj cut off for 36 hours
- **Response:** Local people and police started rescue with bare hands

**Mitigation & Lessons Learned:**
- **Seismic zoning updated:** Kutch moved to Zone V (highest)
- **National Building Code (NBC)** revised and enforced
- **Retrofitting:** Old public buildings (hospitals, schools) strengthened
- **School safety program:** Earthquake drills in all Gujarat schools
- **Early warning system:** Not possible for earthquakes; focus on preparedness

**Do’s & Don’ts (from this disaster):**
- Do construct buildings with earthquake-resistant features (band, columns, foundation)
- Do practice "Drop, Cover, Hold" in schools and offices
- Don't build on soft soil or near fault lines
- Don't use heavy parapets and un-reinforced masonry in high-risk zones

## 9. Kumbakonam School Fire Disaster (2004)

**Introduction:** On 16 July 2004, a fire broke out in the Lord Krishna Middle School in Kumbakonam, Tamil Nadu, killing 94 children (ages 6-13) and injuring 30 others – one of India's deadliest school fires.

**Causes:**
- **Kitchen fire:** School kitchen on ground floor; fire started from stove that had kerosene drip
- **Flammable materials:** The building's thatched roof and wooden structures caught fire instantly
- **No fire exits:** Single narrow staircase; classrooms had no other way out
- **Unsafe building:** School run from old building with no fire safety compliance
- **Panic:** Children rushed toward staircase, fell and suffocated; many jumped from windows

**What Happened:**
- **Timing:** 11:30 AM (during lunch break; kitchen operating)
- **Spread:** Within 10 minutes, entire building engulfed
- **Deaths:** 94 children (charred beyond recognition)
- **Injuries:** 30 hospitalized; many with burns, fractures from jumping
- **Property:** School building totally destroyed

**Before the Fire:**
- No fire safety inspection of school
- No fire extinguisher in the school
- No fire drill or evacuation plan
- Kitchen inside the school without safety separation

**During the Fire:**
- **Response:** Local residents and fire brigade arrived (fire station 5 km away; took 20 minutes)
- **Rescue:** Community broke windows and walls to pull children out
- **Hospital:** Overwhelmed; children with 90% burns died within hours

**After the Fire (Response & Rehabilitation):**
- **Compensation:** ₹2 lakh for deceased families (announced by PM)
- **Free medical treatment** for injured children
- **Legal action:** School headmaster and manager arrested (culpable homicide)
- **Political response:** Tamil Nadu CM and PM visited site

**Government Actions (Post-2004):**
- **Tamil Nadu School Safety Guidelines (2004):** Mandatory fire safety compliance for all schools
- **National Building Code (2005 revision):** Fire safety provisions for school buildings
- **Ban on thatched roofs:** All schools prohibited from using thatched/temporary roofs
- **Mandatory fire extinguishers:** Every school must have fire extinguishers and alarms
- **Fire drills:** Mandatory evacuation drills twice a year
- **Kitchen regulations:** School kitchens must be separate from classrooms, LPG not kerosene

**Lessons Learned:**
- School fires can be prevented with low-cost measures
- Fire safety regulations must be enforced, not just written
- Rural schools need regular inspections
- Community first responders are critical (fire brigade arrived late)

**Do’s & Don’ts:**
- Do have two separate fire exits in every school building
- Do conduct monthly fire drills
- Do keep fire extinguisher on every floor (train staff to use PASS: Pull, Aim, Squeeze, Sweep)
- Don't have thatched roofs or wooden structures in schools
- Don't have kitchen inside the same building without fire separation

## 10. North India Cold Waves (2019-20)

**Introduction:** The winter of December 2019 to January 2020 saw one of the most severe cold waves in North India in over a century, with Delhi recording its coldest December day (1.7°C) in 100 years.

**Causes:**
- **Western disturbances:** Cold winds from Himalayan region (northwesterly) brought sub-zero air to plains
- **Clear skies:** Nighttime radiative cooling dropped temperatures further
- **Fog formation:** Dense cold fog trapped cold air near ground (temperature inversion)
- **Climate variability:** La Niña conditions intensified winter

**What Happened:**
- **Temperatures:** Delhi 1.7°C (Safdarjung); Amritsar -0.5°C; Agra -1°C
- **Cold wave conditions:** 20+ days of temperatures 4-6°C below normal
- **Deaths:** 100+ homeless people died of hypothermia in Delhi alone (total North India >200)
- **Health impact:** Respiratory illness spike (asthma, COPD), heart attacks increased
- **Transport disruption:** 
  - Over 500 flights delayed/diverted (dense fog reduced visibility to 0 meters)
  - 100+ trains delayed; 40+ canceled
  - 50+ road accidents due to fog
- **School closures:** Schools in Delhi, UP, Punjab opened late or closed for days
- **Agriculture:** Potato, tomato, and vegetable crops damaged by frost

**Before the Cold Wave:**
- IMD issued cold wave warnings (but no state-level action plans existed)
- Night shelters for homeless were insufficient in many cities
- No awareness campaigns on hypothermia prevention

**During the Cold Wave:**
- **Government response:**
  - Delhi government opened 200+ night shelters (capacity 30,000)
  - Blankets and warm clothes distributed to homeless
  - Free bonfires at designated spots
  - School timings changed to 10 AM (to avoid morning fog)
  - Health advisories issued (avoid morning walks, cover ears/head)
- **Challenges:** Many homeless refused to go to shelters; some died on streets

**After the Cold Wave:**
- **Compensation:** ₹20,000 per death (ex gratia) to families of homeless victims
- **Crop compensation:** Farmers with frost damage received Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana claims
- **Policy changes:** Cold Wave Action Plans developed for Delhi, Punjab, Haryana, UP

**Government Actions (Post-2019-20):**
- **NDMA Cold Wave Guidelines (2021):** Framework for states
- **Night shelter expansion:** Increased capacity and outreach (mobile vans to pick up homeless)
- **Early warning:** IMD now issues color-coded cold wave alerts (yellow/orange/red)
- **Fog forecasting:** Specialized fog advisory for aviation and railways
- **Healthcare preparedness:** Winter clinics in high-risk areas

**Cold Wave Definition (IMD):**
- Plains: Minimum temperature <10°C and departure 4.5-6.4°C (cold wave); >6.4°C (severe)
- Coastal areas: Minimum <15°C and departure 4.5-6.4°C
- Hills: Minimum <0°C

**Lessons Learned:**
- Cold waves kill as many people as heat waves, but get less attention
- Homeless and elderly are most vulnerable – need targeted outreach
- Fog disrupts transport more than snow in North India
- Night shelters must have adequate capacity and be accessible

**Do’s & Don’ts:**
- Do wear layered woolen clothing; cover head, ears, hands, feet
- Do stay indoors during extreme cold
- Do eat warm, high-energy food and drink warm fluids
- Don't ignore shivering (early sign of hypothermia)
- Don't consume alcohol to "stay warm" (actually increases heat loss)
- Don't use unventilated kerosene or gas heaters indoors (carbon monoxide risk)

## 11. Indian Ocean Earthquakes and Tsunamis (2004)

**Introduction:** On 26 December 2004, a magnitude 9.1-9.3 undersea earthquake off the west coast of Sumatra, Indonesia, triggered a devastating tsunami that killed over 230,000 people across 14 countries, including around 10,000 in India.

**Causes:**
- **Subduction zone earthquake:** Indian Plate subducted beneath Burma Plate along Sunda Trench
- **Vertical displacement:** Seafloor lifted 20+ meters, displacing 30 cubic km of water
- **Shallow depth:** Focus at 30 km – intense energy release
- **No warning system:** Indian Ocean had no tsunami warning system at that time

**What Happened – India:**
- **Earthquake:** Felt in Chennai, Kolkata, Andaman & Nicobar Islands at 6:30 AM IST
- **Tsunami arrival:** Andaman & Nicobar (7 AM); Tamil Nadu (8-9 AM); Kerala (9-10 AM)
- **Wave height:** 10-30 meters (Andaman); 5-10 meters (Tamil Nadu coast); 3-5 meters (Kerala)
- **Deaths in India:** ~10,000 (Tamil Nadu 7,500+, Andaman 3,500+, Kerala 168, Andhra 107, Pondicherry 600+)
- **Displaced:** 5,00,000+ (Tamil Nadu alone)
- **Loss:** Entire fishing villages destroyed; 30,000+ fishermen lost; 50,000+ boats damaged
- **Andaman & Nicobar:** Indira Point (southernmost tip of India) submerged; 10 islands partly submerged

**Before the Tsunami:**
- No early warning system in Indian Ocean
- No public awareness about natural warning signs (earthquake, sea retreating)
- Fishing communities not trained on evacuation

**During the Tsunami:**
- **Natural warning:** Many areas saw sea water recede 500+ meters – some ran toward it (fatal mistake)
- **Response:** Almost no organized response initially; local communities did rescue
- **Communication:** Phones down; coastal villages isolated

**After the Tsunami (Response & Relief – India):**
- **Immediate:** Army, Navy, Air Force, Coast Guard deployed
- **Navy:** 45 ships and 100 aircraft used for rescue and supply
- **Aerial supply:** Food, water, medicine dropped to isolated islands
- **Field hospitals:** Set up in Nagapattinam, Car Nicobar
- **Compensation:** ₹5 lakh per death (fishermen); ₹2 lakh for others
- **Rebuilding:** 50,000+ new houses built for displaced

**Government Actions (Post-2004):**
- **Indian Tsunami Early Warning System (ITEWES):** Established at INCOIS, Hyderabad (2007)
  - Seismic network (real-time)
  - Bottom Pressure Recorders (BPRs) in deep ocean
  - Tide gauges
  - Numerical models
- **Tsunami warnings:** 24×7 monitoring and alert dissemination
- **Community awareness:** Coastal drills, mock evacuations, education on natural signs
- **Mangrove restoration:** Increased funding for coastal bio-shields
- **Building codes:** Tsunami-resistant construction in coastal zones

**Lessons Learned:**
- Tsunami warning system is essential for Indian Ocean region
- Natural signs: Strong earthquake + sea retreating = run to high ground
- Public education saves lives (knowledge of natural signs)
- Fishing communities need special training and communication

**Do’s & Don’ts:**
- Do move to high ground (30 m or 3 km inland) immediately after strong earthquake
- Do watch for sea water retreating from beach (natural warning)
- Don't go to beach to watch tsunami
- Don't assume one wave is end (multiple waves arrive for hours)
- Don't return until officially cleared

## 12. Mumbai Floods (2005)

**Introduction:** On 26 July 2005, Mumbai received unprecedented rainfall of 944 mm in 24 hours (normal for entire July is 800 mm), causing the worst urban floods in the city's history, killing over 1,000 people.

**Causes:**
- **Extreme rainfall:** 944 mm (94.4 cm) – highest single-day rainfall in Indian history at that time
- **Cloudburst-like intensity:** 100 mm fell in just 1 hour (2 PM to 3 PM)
- **High tide:** Flooding coincided with high tide (4.5 m) – water couldn't drain into sea
- **Poor drainage:** Mithi River choked with silt and encroachments; storm drains blocked by solid waste
- **Urbanization:** Impervious surfaces (concrete, asphalt) increased runoff

**What Happened:**
- **Rainfall:** 7 AM 27 July – 944 mm in 24 hours at Santacruz observatory (IMD)
- **Waterlogging:** 10-15 feet (3-4 m) in low-lying areas (Dharavi, Kurla, Mahim, Sion, Hindmata)
- **Casualties:** 1,094 deaths (official) – most due to drowning, electrocution, building collapse
- **Injuries:** 2,000+
- **Displacement:** 2,00,000+ homeless for days
- **Transport collapse:**
  - Railway suspended (track submerged); 1 lakh+ stranded at stations
  - Flights canceled; airport closed (runway flooded)
  - Roads blocked (Bandra-Worli Sea Link incomplete; all bridges choked)
- **Economic loss:** ₹1,000+ crore (business interruption, property damage)
- **Mithi River:** Overflowed its banks; flooded several slum colonies

**Before the Floods:**
- IMD issued heavy rain warning (but not "extreme rainfall" alert – terminology evolved later)
- No action taken to clear Mithi River bed
- Drainage system not upgraded since British era
- No coordinated disaster management plan for Mumbai

**During the Floods (Response):**
- **No organized response initially** – city paralyzed
- **Rescue:** Common citizens, police, fire brigade, volunteers with boats
- **Emergency shelters:** BMC opened 200+ school buildings
- **Communication collapsed:** Phones, internet down for 24+ hours
- **Food/water:** Distributed by NGOs and BMC from second day
- **Challenges:** Rescuers couldn't reach many areas; helicopters couldn't land

**After the Floods (Relief & Rehabilitation):**
- **Compensation:** ₹2 lakh for deceased families (BMC + government)
- **Free food & water** for 10+ days in affected wards
- **Relief camps:** Functioned for 2-3 weeks; later shifted to temporary housing
- **Disease control:** Chlorination of wells; no major outbreak
- **Compensation to shopkeepers and vehicle owners**

**Government Actions (Post-2005):**
- **Mithi River clean-up:** Desilting and widening; removal of encroachments
- **Storm drainage upgrade:** 
  - New pumping stations (Brihanmumbai Storm Water Disposal System – Brimstowad)
  - ₹1,500 crore project (ongoing)
- **Early warning system:** 
  - Automatic weather stations
  - Real-time water level sensors in nullahs
  - SMS alerts to citizens
- **Disaster Management Cell:** Created in BMC with 24×7 control room
- **Flood inundation maps:** GIS-based mapping of vulnerable areas
- **Building codes:** No construction within 50 m of Mithi River
- **IMD upgrade:** "Red alert" for 944 mm event now triggers emergency response

**Lessons Learned:**
- Urban flooding is different from river flooding – drainage capacity is critical
- High tide + heavy rain = extreme vulnerability in coastal cities
- Encroachments on rivers and drains worsen floods exponentially
- Communication redundancy needed (mobile towers failed)
- Citizens must be trained to stay put during extreme rainfall (avoid traveling)

**Do’s & Don’ts (from 2005 Mumbai Floods):**
- Do stay indoors during extreme rainfall warnings
- Do keep emergency supplies (food, water, torch, power bank) during monsoon
- Don't travel unless absolutely necessary (trains and roads flood quickly)
- Don't walk or drive through waterlogged streets (manholes open, electrocution risk)
- Don't stay in low-lying basements during heavy rain

**Urban Flood Runoff Formula:**
```
Q = C × I × A
Q = Peak runoff rate (cumecs)
C = Runoff coefficient (0.3 barren land, 0.7-0.9 urban)
I = Rainfall intensity (mm/hr)
A = Catchment area (km²)
```

```
MO = rigidity × area × slip
Moment Magnitude (Mw) = 2/3 log10(MO) - 10.7
```
